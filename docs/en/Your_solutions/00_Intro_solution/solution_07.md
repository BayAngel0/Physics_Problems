### Interpretation & Key Takeaway

This problem is a classic example of how a **shift in perspective** can drastically simplify a physical model. 

If we try to calculate the distance of each individual flight back and forth, we are forced to set up an infinite geometric series to track the ever-decreasing segments. While mathematically possible, it is inefficient. However, by shifting our focus from **distance** to **time**, the problem becomes trivial.

**The Core Logic:**
1. **Simultaneous Motion:** The fly and the bicycle start moving at the same time, and the scenario ends the exact moment the bicycle hits the wall.
2. **The Time Constraint:** The total time the fly spends flying is entirely dictated by the bicycle's travel time ($t = d/v = 10 \text{ s}$).
3. **Continuous Speed:** The fly travels at a constant speed ($2 \text{ m/s}$) for this entire $10$-second duration. Its changes in direction do not affect the total scalar distance covered.
4. **Final Calculation:** Therefore, the total distance is simply $d = v \cdot t = 20 \text{ m}$.

**Exam Note:** When faced with a complex oscillating or repeating process, always check if there is a unifying constraint (like total time) that binds the whole system together before attempting to calculate individual parts.
